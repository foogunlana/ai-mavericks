AI Mavericks - Q1 2026 Dinner Recap

Recap of many of the things we covered during our Q1 2026 gathering at Pizza Express Live Holborn.

Eddie Forson
March 21, 2026

Hey guys,

Great seeing you at our Q1 2026 dinner! Once again, I’m a bit late in sending this dinner recap. I promise to get this recap to you earlier next time around.

As usual, let’s start with bios first!

Guests

Bode describes himself as an "AI-holic". He's CTO at Stears, a financial data platform, where he's making proprietary datasets conversational through LLMs. He's particularly interested in generative UI: letting the AI decide how to present data rather than building fixed dashboards. Speak to him about financial data, conversational interfaces, and generative UI.

David is a computer scientist with a PhD who spent 10 years as a university lecturer, with a background in game design. He now runs a consultancy with two subcontractors, juggling rapid prototyping engagements, a RAG system for a university, and a major gig as architect for Pearson's AI-first education platform. Speak to him about AI in education, rapid prototyping, RAG architectures, and teaching people to use AI tools.

Farhath is a full-stack engineer currently freelancing. He's building an AI media project focused on product photography, using image generation models and compositing techniques to create studio-quality shots without the studio. Farhath is also active on the hackathon circuit. Speak to him about creative AI, image generation for commercial use, and the hackathon scene.

Liberatus comes from a product management background and runs a two-person AI-native agency. His main project is Reps, an AI sports coach deployed across web, iOS, as a Claude Code skill, and as an MCP app. His core thesis: the interface doesn't matter, the engine does. Go where the users are. Speak to him about multi-platform AI deployment, AI-native agency building, and interface-agnostic product strategy.

Neil s building Response Hub: an AI product that helps companies handle security questionnaires faster. Before that, he was co-founding CTO at Progression (a career development tool that was sold at the end of 2024). Neil's now transitioning into consulting alongside the product work. Speak to him about enterprise sales tooling, AI product development, and the founder-to-consultant journey.

Rene is a PM consultant and CTO for Professors Without Borders, a charity connecting academics globally. He co-runs the Hugging Face London meetup and has been exploring compliance challenges in agentic AI systems. Speak to him about AI governance, agentic compliance, community building, and the charity tech space.

Eddie (me). Software engineer by trade, currently working as a contractor CTO for a boutique reinsurance consultancy. On the side I'm building Ghana News AI; think Bloomberg-style news and market intelligence for Ghana and the African market. At work I've been pushing my teams to go AI-native this year, which has been equal parts rewarding and humbling. Happy to talk about AI adoption in teams, news aggregation, and the African tech market.

Key discussion points

  • AI and the future of software engineering:

    • A big theme this quarter — should junior developers even bother learning traditional coding? The group was split, but the consensus leaned towards: the hard part was never writing code. It's requirements gathering, understanding what to build, and navigating ambiguity.

    • We're moving up abstraction levels fast. The people who thrive will be the ones who can articulate problems clearly, not necessarily the ones who can write the tightest loops.

    • Interesting read on Killing the Code Review

  • The identity crisis — craftsperson vs builder:

    • This one got personal. AI genuinely threatens people who love the craft of coding for its own sake. If you find joy in elegant algorithms and clean architecture, watching an AI spit out "good enough" code in seconds is unsettling.

    • But if you love building things and shipping outcomes, AI is the best thing that's ever happened to you. David flagged resistance in the gaming industry specifically, e.g. studios pushing back hard on AI-generated assets.

  • Rapid prototyping and the shifting consulting landscape:

    • David's consultancy has shifted to two-week delivery cycles. Clients get a working prototype fast, and the big upfront discovery phase is shrinking or disappearing entirely. When you can prototype in two weeks, the traditional PM-heavy, scoping-heavy engagement model starts to look bloated.

    • Speed is becoming the competitive advantage. Several consultants around the table are adapting too. The old model of months-long scoping followed by months-long builds feels increasingly outdated when you can show something tangible in a fortnight.

    • Networking remains the primary path to work. Every person at the dinner had found their current engagements through personal connections rather than inbound marketing or cold outreach.

  • AI economics and the SaaS paradox:

    • Neil raised a sharp point: AI app unit economics actually get worse at scale, not better. Every additional user costs real money in inference. That's the opposite of traditional SaaS.

    • We're in a subsidised inference era right now. The big model providers are eating costs to grab market share. When that ends, a lot of AI startups will face uncomfortable conversations about pricing.

    • Broader shift from growth-narrative valuations to results-based valuations feels overdue.

  • Multi-platform product strategy:

    • Liberatus walked us through his approach with Reps: same AI engine, deployed as a web app, iOS app, Claude Code skill, and MCP integration. Four interfaces, one brain.

    • His thesis is the following: the interface is commoditised; the engine and the data are what matter. Go where your users already are rather than forcing them to come to you.

    • The group got excited about generative UI as the next step: let the AI decide the best way to present information rather than designing fixed screens.

  • AI in education:

    • David's work on Pearson's AI-first education platform sparked a big conversation. The vision: workforce analytics, personalised learning paths, and AI tutoring at scale.

    • His university RAG project highlighted how institutions are starting to make their knowledge bases accessible through conversational interfaces.

    • He raised an interesting generational split: younger students adopt AI tools almost instantly, while established academics are more cautious.

  • AI tool adoption in teams:

    • Several of us shared experiences pushing Claude Code adoption in our organisations. The speed improvements are real, but getting people to change their workflows is the actual challenge.

    • David's approach of teaching Claude Code directly to teams seems to be working well. Hands-on training beats documentation every time.

    • This study from Anthropic is worth the read: https://www.anthropic.com/features/81k-interviews

  • Image generation and creative AI:

    • Farhath's product photography project is a good example of where AI is genuinely replacing expensive processes - a studio shoot that would cost thousands can now be approximated with image models and compositing.

    • The gaming industry backlash came up again here. There's a real cultural resistance to AI-generated art in gaming communities that doesn't exist in commercial photography.

  • Financial data and market intelligence:

    • Bode's work at Stears — making proprietary financial data conversational — connects to something I'm doing with Ghana News AI. The common thread is: structured data becomes dramatically more valuable when you can ask it questions in plain English.

    • Generative UI for financial dashboards could be genuinely transformative. Instead of building 50 dashboard views, let the AI generate the right visualisation for each query.

Closing notes

See you around.
Eddie.
